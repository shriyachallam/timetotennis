A student interview
MAY 6, 2020

What makes tennis such a unique sport is the development of tennis etiquette over the years the sport has been played. It's just as important to players as it is to spectators and parents. Although following these unspoken rules may sound old-fashioned, they are the best way to ensure everyone enjoys the game thoroughly.

FOR PLAYERS:
One of the best ways to show your sportsmanship is to win the match/point in a humble manner and lose it in graceful fashion. This can also be seen at the end of the match when you meet your opponent at the net and shake hands, no matter what the outcome was.

During the game, ensure to silence your cell phone and steer clear of any visual or verbal distractions. This can include unnecessary talking/noises during a play or intentionally distracting movements to fluster your opponent. These can be especially misleading since it may seem like you are calling a ball out, therefore lead to confusion between you and your opponent. Speaking of outs, line calls are important to make in the most fair manner possible and it is important you give your opponent the benefit of the doubt if you are not completely sure of where the ball landed.

When beginning the match, make sure to keep a short, professional warm up, followed by warm up serves, and the spin of the racket to decide who will serve first and what side of the net each player will start. When the points start, it is crucial to call out the score on your serve, in a loud enough manner to avoid any future confusion.

FOR SPECTATORS:
As a spectator, the best way to encourage the players is to stay silent during the matches so you are not a distraction to the players, or other spectators around you. With this said, it is encouraging to cheer for a player, but only after the point is over. There will be times, such as when players are switching sides, when it is acceptable to get out of your seat to stretch, etc.

Additionally, make sure to keep your phone on silent and if recording/using your camera, make sure you are considerate of your surroundings and not blocking anyone's view. Finally, certain international competitions such as the US Open, Wimbeledon, Australian Open, and French Open may have some of their own specific dress codes.

FOR PARENTS:
Parents follow similar etiquette as spectators, but it is always a reminder to not let your emotions get in the way of your child's playing to an extreme where it can harm them or their opponent. Passive cheering or coaching your child is strongly advised against, as it can be a distraction for others around you and the players, as well.
