Types of serves
June 8, 2020

Serving in tennis is one of the hardest parts of the game, but staying motivated to improve it, can make it one of the most rewarding advantages over your opponent. It is the only shot where you possess all the power, in terms of its timing and execution, since all other shots are reactive and dependent on your opponent. There are four main types of serves: the flat serve, slice serve, kick serve, and underhand serve.
Players may use a combination of these serves and may reserve certain types of serves for their second serve, while leaving the powerful ones for their first serves.

FLAT SERVE:
The flat serve is played out with a Continental grip and the swing goes directly through the ball with no spin. Although there is a low margin of error, the advantage of this serve is that the combination of the low bounce (due to the no spin) and the fast speed leads to a difficult ball to return for your opponent. It is essential that this serve has a 12-18 inches toss in front of you and 6 inches to the right of your tossing arm’s shoulder at its peak. The contact point will occur with a direct strike, rather than brushing up on the ball or hitting it on the side.

SLICE SERVE:
This serve is very natural to hit once it is perfected, especially due to the use of the Continental grip. This is a very diverse serve as it can be used to open up the court (if hitting from right to left, due to the natural spin) and it can also be used to crowd your opponent (if hitting to the center of the service box). This has a similar toss placement as a flat serve, but instead of a direct strike, you want to make contact with the outer edge of the ball to properly execute the sidespin motion. At the same time, you want to give slight topspin so it drops back down to the court.

KICK SERVE:
The kicker serve is the default second serve for a lot of players since it provides a lot of control for the player and is very precise. Additionally, it also has a high bounce due to the heavy topspin, which can make it difficult to return. But, what makes it a little easier to return is the slow bounce, allowing your opponent to have more reaction time. Again, this requires a Continental grip but the toss is different as it directly above your head and even slightly behind you to come from under the ball. This is to ensure there is the right spin with the brush up technique.

UNDERHAND SERVE:
As the name suggests, the underhanded serve happens below shoulder level. This is one of the rarest serves and is practically never used, but it is still legal as long as the ball is hit prior to hitting the ground.
